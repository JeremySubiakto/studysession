Quantitative Reasoning

Quant, from the ground up

The Focus edition tests arithmetic, algebra, word problems, number properties, and statistics — and no geometry. No calculator is allowed, so we build number sense alongside the rules.

1 · Arithmetic foundations 2 · Percents, ratios & proportions 3 · Number properties 4 · Algebra 5 · Word problems 6 · Statistics & probability
1

Arithmetic foundations

Everything in Quant rests on confident arithmetic: the number line, order of operations, fractions, and decimals. Let us rebuild it cleanly.

The number line & integers

Integers are the whole numbers and their negatives: …, −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, … They sit on a line, negatives to the left of zero, positives to the right. The absolute value |x| is the distance from zero, so |−4| = 4 and |4| = 4.

Sign rules Same signs multiply/divide to a positive; different signs give a negative. (−)(−)=+, (−)(+)=−. Subtracting a negative adds: 5 − (−3) = 8.

Order of operations (PEMDAS)

RuleParentheses → Exponents → Multiply/Divide (left→right) → Add/Subtract (left→right)
Worked example Evaluate 12 − 3 × 2² + (8 ÷ 4)
  1. Parentheses first: (8 ÷ 4) = 2. Now 12 − 3 × 2² + 2.
  2. Exponents: 2² = 4. Now 12 − 3 × 4 + 2.
  3. Multiplication: 3 × 4 = 12. Now 12 − 12 + 2.
  4. Left to right addition/subtraction: 12 − 12 = 0, then 0 + 2 = 2.

Fractions

A fraction is part / whole. To add or subtract, you need a common denominator. To multiply, multiply across. To divide, multiply by the reciprocal (flip the second fraction).

Formulaa/b + c/d = (ad + bc) / bd a/b × c/d = ac/bd a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c
Worked example Compute 3/4 + 2/3
  1. Common denominator of 4 and 3 is 12.
  2. Rewrite: 3/4 = 9/12 and 2/3 = 8/12.
  3. Add the numerators: 9/12 + 8/12 = 17/12.
  4. 17/12 is improper; as a mixed number it is 1 5/12.
Worked example Compute 3/5 ÷ 9/10
  1. Dividing means multiply by the reciprocal: 3/5 × 10/9.
  2. Multiply across: (3 × 10) / (5 × 9) = 30/45.
  3. Simplify by dividing top and bottom by 15: 2/3.

Decimals & conversions

Memorizing common fraction–decimal–percent equivalents saves real time (no calculator!): 1/2 = 0.5 = 50%, 1/4 = 0.25 = 25%, 1/5 = 0.2 = 20%, 1/8 = 0.125 = 12.5%, 1/3 ≈ 0.333 = 33⅓%.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Doing addition before multiplication — multiplication always comes first.
  • Adding fractions by adding both tops and bottoms (3/4 + 2/3 ≠ 5/7). You must find a common denominator.
  • Forgetting that subtracting a negative is the same as adding.
2

Percents, ratios & proportions

Percent simply means "per 100." So 25% = 25/100 = 0.25. The single most important question to keep asking is: percent of what? The base can change inside a problem.

Formulasx% of N = (x/100) × N Percent change = (new − old) / old × 100% Successive percents MULTIPLY: +25% then −20% → × 1.25 × 0.80
Worked example A $80 coat is marked up 25%, then discounted 20%. Final price?
  1. Markup: 80 × 1.25 = 100.
  2. Discount off the new price: 100 × 0.80 = 80.
  3. Final price is $80. The percentages do not simply cancel — it is a coincidence of these numbers.

Ratios & proportions

A ratio compares quantities: 3 : 5 means "3 parts to 5 parts." A proportion sets two ratios equal; solve by cross-multiplying. To combine A : B with B : C, scale them so the shared term B matches.

RuleIf a/b = c/d, then a·d = b·c (cross-multiply)
Worked example If A : B = 3 : 5 and B : C = 2 : 7, find A : B : C
  1. The shared term B is 5 in the first ratio and 2 in the second. Their LCM is 10.
  2. Scale A : B = 3 : 5 up by 2 → 6 : 10.
  3. Scale B : C = 2 : 7 up by 5 → 10 : 35.
  4. Now B matches at 10, so A : B : C = 6 : 10 : 35.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Reading 0.2% as 0.2 or 2%. 0.2% = 0.002.
  • Reversing a percent wrongly: if x is 30% of y, then y is 333⅓% of x (divide by 0.3), not 70% or 300%.
  • Stacking ratios side by side without matching the shared term first.
3

Number properties

These questions test how integers behave: factors, primes, even/odd, divisibility, and remainders. Little computation, lots of logic.

Factors, multiples & primes

A factor divides a number evenly; a multiple is the number times an integer. A prime has exactly two factors, 1 and itself (2, 3, 5, 7, 11, …). 2 is the only even prime. Every integer breaks into a unique product of primes — its prime factorization.

Worked example Prime-factorize 84
  1. 84 is even: 84 = 2 × 42.
  2. 42 = 2 × 21.
  3. 21 = 3 × 7, both prime.
  4. So 84 = 2² × 3 × 7.

Divisibility shortcuts

Divisible byTest

3digit sum is divisible by 3
4last two digits divisible by 4
5ends in 0 or 5

Even / odd (parity)

Ruleseven ± even = even odd ± odd = even even ± odd = odd even × anything = even odd × odd = odd
Powerful fact The product of two consecutive integers n(n+1) is always even, because one of any two consecutive integers is even.

Remainders

Worked example What is the remainder when 7⁴ is divided by 5?
  1. Replace 7 with its remainder mod 5: 7 leaves remainder 2.
  2. So 7⁴ has the same remainder as 2⁴ = 16.
  3. 16 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 1. Answer: 1. (Check: 7⁴ = 2401 = 5×480 + 1.)
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting 1 is not prime and 2 is the only even prime.
  • Assuming n² is always odd or always even — it depends on n. Test small cases (0, 1, 2, −1).
  • Computing huge powers directly instead of reducing the base by its remainder first.
4

Algebra

Algebra is arithmetic with unknowns. Translate the words into one variable when you can; only add a second variable if you also have a second equation.

Solving linear equations

Do the same thing to both sides until the variable is alone. Distribute carefully across parentheses.

Worked example Solve 3(x − 2) = 2x + 5
  1. Distribute the 3 to both terms: 3x − 6 = 2x + 5.
  2. Subtract 2x from both sides: x − 6 = 5.
  3. Add 6: x = 11.

Systems of equations

Two equations, two unknowns. Elimination: add or subtract to cancel a variable. Substitution: solve one for a variable and plug in.

Worked example Solve x + y = 10 and x − y = 4
  1. Add the two equations; the y's cancel: 2x = 14.
  2. So x = 7.
  3. Substitute back: 7 + y = 10 → y = 3. Solution: x = 7, y = 3.

Exponents

Rulesxᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ xᵃ / xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ x⁰ = 1 x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ
Worked example If 2ˣ = 32, find x
  1. Write 32 as a power of 2: 32 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 2⁵.
  2. So 2ˣ = 2⁵, meaning the exponents match.
  3. x = 5.

Quadratics & factoring

Patternsx² + (p+q)x + pq = (x + p)(x + q) a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b) Quadratic formula: x = [ −b ± √(b² − 4ac) ] / 2a
Worked example Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0
  1. Find two numbers that multiply to +6 and add to −5: those are −2 and −3.
  2. Factor: (x − 2)(x − 3) = 0.
  3. Set each factor to zero: x = 2 or x = 3. The roots are 2 and 3 (the values that make a factor zero — note the sign flips from the factors).

Inequalities

The one rule everyone forgets Multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number flips the inequality sign.
Worked example Solve −3x + 2 > 11
  1. Subtract 2 from both sides: −3x > 9.
  2. Divide by −3 — and flip the sign: x < −3.
  3. Answer: x < −3.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Not distributing to every term inside parentheses.
  • Confusing the factors (x−2)(x−3) with the roots (2 and 3) — the roots have the opposite sign of the numbers inside.
  • Forgetting to flip the inequality when dividing by a negative.

Rate · time = distance, and work

FormulasDistance = rate × time Combined work rate = 1/a + 1/b (rates ADD; times do not)
Worked example A train goes 180 miles in 3 hours. How far in 5 hours at that rate?
  1. Rate = distance ÷ time = 180 ÷ 3 = 60 mph.
  2. Distance in 5 hours = rate × time = 60 × 5.
  3. = 300 miles.
Worked example Pump A fills a tank in 4 h, pump B in 6 h. Together?
  1. Rates add: 1/4 + 1/6 of the tank per hour.
  2. Common denominator 12: 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12 per hour.
  3. Time = 1 ÷ (5/12) = 12/5 = 2.4 hours — faster than either alone, as it must be.

Mixtures & averages

FormulaAverage = sum ÷ count → sum = average × count
Worked example How much pure water to add to 10 L of 40% acid to make it 25% acid?
  1. Track the acid — it does not change: 40% of 10 = 4 L of acid.
  2. We want that 4 L to be 25% of the new total T: 4 = 0.25 × T → T = 16 L.
  3. Water added = 16 − 10 = 6 liters.

Overlapping sets

FormulaTotal = A + B − (both) + (neither)
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Averaging rates or times instead of adding rates in work problems.
  • Tracking percentages in mixtures rather than the actual quantity of the substance.
  • Forgetting to subtract the overlap in set problems (double-counting).

Statistics & probability

Center & spread

DefinitionsMean = sum ÷ count Median = middle value when sorted (average the two middle if even count) Mode = most frequent value Range = max − min

Standard deviation measures how spread out values are around the mean. Identical values have SD = 0; more spread means a larger SD. You will compare, not compute, it.

Worked example Find the median of {3, 7, 2, 9, 5}
  1. Sort first: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9.
  2. Five values → the middle (3rd) one is the median.
  3. Median = 5. (The mean is 26/5 = 5.2 — don't confuse them.)

Probability

RulesP(event) = favorable ÷ total P(A and B), independent = P(A) × P(B) P(A or B), mutually exclusive = P(A) + P(B) P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
Worked example A fair coin is flipped twice. P(two heads)?
  1. Each flip is independent, P(head) = 1/2.
  2. P(head AND head) = 1/2 × 1/2.
  3. = 1/4.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Reporting the mean when asked for the median (or forgetting to sort first).
  • Thinking bigger numbers automatically mean a bigger standard deviation — it's about spread.
  • Adding probabilities of independent events instead of multiplying.

Verbal Reasoning

Verbal, made logical

Two question types: Critical Reasoning and Reading Comprehension. (The Focus edition removed Sentence Correction.) No outside knowledge is needed — only precise reading and clear reasoning.

1 · How Verbal works 2 · Critical Reasoning 3 · Reading Comprehension
1

How Verbal works

You face short argument passages (Critical Reasoning) and longer reading passages (Reading Comprehension). Both reward the same habit: read for structure, not for memory. Identify what the author is claiming and how the pieces support it.

The golden rule The correct answer is always provable from the text. If you must bring in your own assumptions or world knowledge to defend a choice, it is wrong.

Wrong answers fall into recognizable buckets: too extreme (always, never, only), out of scope, half-right then half-wrong, or true-but-irrelevant. Naming the flaw helps you eliminate fast.

2

Critical Reasoning

Every argument has three parts. Find the conclusion first — the point the author wants you to accept. The evidence (premises) supports it. Bridging them is an unstated assumption.

StructureEvidence + (Assumption) → Conclusion

The question types

TypeYour job
AssumptionFind the missing link the argument needs. Test by negating it — if the argument falls apart, that's the assumption.
StrengthenAdd support, confirm the assumption, or rule out an alternative cause.
WeakenBreak the link — usually by offering an alternative explanation. You needn't disprove the conclusion, just make it less likely.
InferencePick what must be true from the statements. Stay close to the text; avoid extremes.
Flaw / EvaluateName the reasoning error, or find what would test the argument.
The GMAT's favorite trap Treating correlation as causation. "B happened after A, so A caused B" ignores other possible causes. Most Weaken answers exploit exactly this.
Worked example The negation test for an assumption
  1. Argument: "Company X will profit by moving manufacturing overseas, where labor costs are 40% lower."
  2. Conclusion = it will profit. Evidence = labor is cheaper. The gap: cheaper labor must actually reach the bottom line.
  3. Candidate assumption: "Other costs (shipping, tariffs, quality) won't offset the labor savings."
  4. Negate it: "Other costs WILL offset the savings." Now the argument collapses — so this is a required assumption. Negation breaks it = it's the assumption.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Picking an answer that is true in the real world but not supported by this argument.
  • Choosing a strong, sweeping statement when the argument only needs a modest one.
  • On Weaken/Strengthen, ignoring the conclusion and reacting to the evidence alone.
3

Reading Comprehension

Don't try to memorize details. As you read, build a mental map: What is the main point? What is each paragraph doing? Is the author neutral, critical, or advocating? Then return to the text for specifics.

QuestionApproach
Main ideaChoose the option covering the whole passage and matching the author's tone (rarely extreme).
DetailGo back and find the line. Never answer from memory.
InferenceUnstated but directly supported. Avoid choices that reach beyond the text.
Function ("why mention X?")Answer with the role the detail plays, not what it literally says.
Tone words matter Note whether the author endorses, criticizes, or merely reports. A correct main-idea answer almost never overstates the author's stance.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Picking a choice that's true of one paragraph as the "main idea" of the whole passage.
  • Choosing answers with extreme words (all, never, proves) when the passage is measured.
  • Answering detail questions from memory instead of rereading the relevant line.

2

Data Sufficiency — the discipline

This format confuses people because you are not solving for a number — you are deciding whether each statement gives enough to answer. The five answer choices never change. Memorize them:

The 5 answers(A) Statement (1) ALONE is sufficient, (2) is not. (B) Statement (2) ALONE is sufficient, (1) is not. (C) BOTH together are sufficient, NEITHER alone. (D) EACH alone is sufficient. (E) Together they are STILL not sufficient.
Method Evaluate each statement on its own first — never carry information from statement 1 into statement 2. For a yes/no question, "sufficient" means it always gives the same answer (even if that answer is always "no"). For a value question, it must pin down exactly one value.
Worked example Is x > 0? (1) x² > 0 (2) x³ > 0
  1. Statement (1): x² > 0 only tells you x ≠ 0. x could be +3 or −3 → can't answer. Not sufficient.
  2. Statement (2): x³ > 0. A negative cubed is negative, so x must be positive. Always "yes" → sufficient.
  3. (2) alone works, (1) doesn't → answer (B).
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Treating x² > 0 as "x is positive" — squaring hides the sign.
  • Letting information from statement 1 leak into your read of statement 2.
  • Jumping to (C) without checking whether each statement works alone first.
3

Tables, graphs & multi-source

These give you data and ask you to read it carefully. The trap is rarely the math — it's misreading the chart or grabbing the wrong row.

  • Always read the axes, units, and labels first. Is it dollars or thousands of dollars? Percent or count?
  • Normalize before comparing. "Per rep," "per capita," and percentage change can flip a ranking versus raw totals.
  • Correlation isn't causation. A chart showing two things rising together does not prove one causes the other.
  • Multi-source: check whether one tab alone answers the question before assuming you need both.
Worked example Reading a discount + shipping rule (multi-source thinking)
  1. Rule: 10% code applies to the subtotal first; orders of $50 or less then pay $6 shipping; over $50 ship free.
  2. Cart subtotal = $52. Apply the code: 52 × 0.90 = $46.80.
  3. $46.80 is now ≤ $50, so the $6 fee applies: 46.80 + 6 = $52.80.
  4. Total = $52.80. The discount pushed the order back under the free-shipping line — you must use both facts.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Comparing raw totals when the question asks about a rate or percentage.
  • Misreading units on an axis (thousands vs. ones).
  • Assuming you need both sources before checking whether one settles it.

Strategy & test day

Structure, pacing, and the habits that actually move scores — plus context for the Booth/Columbia goal.

The exam at a glance

The GMAT Focus Edition runs 2 hours 15 minutes: three sections of 45 minutes each — Quantitative (21 questions), Verbal (23), and Data Insights (20). The total score scale is 205–805. You may take the sections in any order, with one optional break.

A standout feature: you can bookmark questions and edit up to three answers per section at the end. Leave a little time to use it.

Pacing

  • Quant: 45 ÷ 21 ≈ 2:08 per question.
  • Verbal: 45 ÷ 23 ≈ 1:57 per question — reading passages eat into this, so read efficiently.
  • Data Insights: 45 ÷ 20 = 2:15, but multi-part items run long, so move fast on single ones.

It's computer-adaptive, and unanswered questions hurt you. Never leave one blank — guess and move on. Don't sink five minutes into a single problem.

The error log — your highest-leverage habit

For every question you miss or guess, record four things: the question, the topic, why you got it wrong (concept gap? careless? timing? misread?), and the correct approach. Review weekly. Most score gains come from killing repeated error types, not from grinding new questions. This app tracks your accuracy automatically — but you write down the why.

Because medians and test policies change every cycle, confirm each school's current class profile and any GRE/test-optional policies directly on their admissions pages before setting a target. Clear the median comfortably, then pour energy into the rest of the application.

Quantitative Methods

The toolkit behind every other topic: how to measure return, value cash flows over time, describe data, reason about uncertainty, and test ideas with statistics.

1 · Rates & returns 2 · Time value of money 3 · Statistical measures 4 · Probability 5 · Distributions & sampling 6 · Hypothesis testing & regression
1

Rates & returns

An interest rate can be read three equivalent ways: a required rate of return, a discount rate that converts future money to today, or an opportunity cost of spending now. A market interest rate stacks up from building blocks.

required return = real risk-free + inflation premium + default + liquidity + maturity premiums
(1 + nominal) = (1 + real)(1 + inflation)

Measuring a single period

HPR = P1 − P0 + incomeP0
Worked example Holding period return
  1. Buy a share at P0 = $50; it pays a $2 dividend; you sell at P1 = $55.
  2. HPR = (55 − 50 + 2) / 50 = 7 / 50.
  3. = 14%.

Averaging returns over time

The arithmetic mean is the simple average. The geometric mean is the true compound growth rate and is always ≤ the arithmetic mean (equal only if every return is identical).

geometric mean = [ (1+R1)(1+R2)…(1+Rn) ]1/n − 1
Worked example Why +50% then −50% is not break-even
  1. Arithmetic mean = (50% + (−50%)) / 2 = 0%.
  2. But $100 → $150 → $75. You actually lost money.
  3. Geometric = √(1.50 × 0.50) − 1 = √0.75 − 1 ≈ −13.4% — the honest compound return.

Money-weighted vs time-weighted

The money-weighted return (MWR) is the IRR of the portfolio's cash flows — it's affected by when you add or withdraw money. The time-weighted return (TWR) chains sub-period returns and removes the effect of cash-flow timing, so it's the standard for judging a manager's skill.

To annualize a sub-period return, compound it: annualized = (1 + period return)periods/year − 1. The continuously compounded return is ln(1 + HPR).

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Quoting the arithmetic mean as the growth you actually earned over multiple periods — use the geometric mean.
  • Comparing managers with money-weighted returns; client deposits/withdrawals distort it. Use time-weighted.
  • Annualizing by simply multiplying (×4 a quarterly return) instead of compounding.
2

Time value of money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because it can earn a return. Everything in valuation is moving cash flows across time with one discount rate.

FV = PV(1 + r)n   PV = FV(1 + r)n
PV annuity = A · 1 − (1 + r)−nr
perpetuity = Ar   growing perpetuity = A1r − g
Worked example Future value of a lump sum
  1. Invest $1,000 at 6% for 3 years, compounded annually.
  2. FV = 1,000 × (1.06)3 = 1,000 × 1.191016.
  3. = $1,191.02.
Worked example Valuing a perpetuity (preferred stock)
  1. A preferred share pays $5 per year forever; investors require 8%.
  2. Value = A / r = 5 / 0.08.
  3. = $62.50. A growing dividend would use A1/(r − g), the Gordon model.
NPV & IRR NPV discounts all project cash flows at the required return and sums them; accept if NPV > 0. The IRR is the rate that makes NPV = 0. Cash flows are additive — you can value pieces separately and add.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Mismatching the rate and the period — a monthly cash flow needs a monthly rate and n in months.
  • Confusing an ordinary annuity (payments at period end) with an annuity due (payments at the start).

Statistical measures of returns

We describe a return series by its center (mean, median, mode) and its spread (variance, standard deviation). Spread is risk.

sample variance s2 = Σ(xi − x)2n − 1   SD = √s2
CV = SDmean   Sharpe = Rp − Rfσp
correlation ρ = cov(x,y)σx σy,  −1 ≤ ρ ≤ +1

Sample vs population: a sample variance divides by n − 1 (not n) to correct for using an estimate of the mean. The coefficient of variation measures risk per unit of return — lower is better. The Sharpe ratio measures excess return per unit of risk — higher is better.

Shape: skewness & kurtosis

Skewness measures asymmetry. Kurtosis measures tail-heaviness: leptokurtic (fat tails, excess kurtosis > 0) means more extreme outcomes than a normal distribution — important for risk. Excess kurtosis = kurtosis − 3.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Dividing by n for a sample variance — use n − 1.
  • Judging an investment by return alone; use CV or Sharpe to account for risk.
  • Reversing skew direction — the skew is named for the side with the long tail.

Worked example Expected return
  1. Outcomes: +20% (prob 0.3), +5% (prob 0.5), −10% (prob 0.2).
  2. E(R) = 0.3(20) + 0.5(5) + 0.2(−10) = 6 + 2.5 − 2.
  3. = 6.5%.

Portfolio mathematics

E(Rp) = w1E(R1) + w2E(R2)

The diversification engine Because of that last term, whenever the correlation ρ < +1, portfolio risk is less than the weighted average of the parts. Lower correlation → more risk reduction. This single idea underlies all of portfolio theory.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the 2·w1w2·cov term in portfolio variance.
  • Treating dependent events as independent (multiplying probabilities that aren't independent).
  • Averaging the standard deviations of two assets — risk does not average unless ρ = +1.

Distributions & sampling

The normal distribution is symmetric and fully described by its mean and variance. The empirical rule: about 68% of outcomes lie within ±1 SD, 95% within ±2, and 99.7% within ±3.

z = x − μσ
confidence interval = x ± z · s√n

Asset prices are often modeled as lognormal (can't go below zero, right-skewed) while continuously compounded returns are modeled as normal. Roy's safety-first criterion picks the portfolio that maximizes the safety-first ratio (E(R) − threshold) / σ, i.e. the one least likely to fall below a minimum return.

Sampling & the Central Limit Theorem

The CLT says the distribution of the sample mean approaches normal as n grows (rule of thumb n ≥ 30), regardless of the population's shape. The spread of that sample mean is the standard error, σ/√n, which shrinks as the sample grows. Common z-values: 1.65 (90%), 1.96 (95%), 2.58 (99%). Use the t-distribution for small samples or unknown variance.

Worked example A 95% confidence interval
  1. Sample mean return 8%, sample SD 10%, n = 100.
  2. Standard error = 10% / √100 = 1%.
  3. 95% CI = 8% ± 1.96 × 1% = 6.04% to 9.96%.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Confusing the standard deviation (spread of data) with the standard error (spread of the sample mean).
  • Using z when the sample is small and variance unknown — use t.
  • Assuming the CLT applies for tiny samples from a non-normal population.

Hypothesis testing & regression

Hypothesis testing is how we decide whether data support a claim. State a null (H0, the status quo) and an alternative (Ha), pick a significance level α, compute a test statistic, and decide.

DecisionH0 is trueH0 is false
Reject H0Type I error (prob α)Correct (power = 1 − β)
Fail to rejectCorrectType II error (prob β)

The p-value is the smallest significance level at which you'd reject H0; reject when p < α. Use a one-tailed test for directional claims, two-tailed otherwise.

Simple linear regression

Y = b0 + b1X + ε
SST = SSR + SSE   R2 = SSRSST

The slope b1 says how much Y changes per one-unit change in X. R² is the fraction of Y's variation explained by X (0 to 1). The ANOVA F-test assesses the model overall; a t-test assesses the slope.

Fintech & Big Data (brief) "Big Data" is described by volume, velocity, variety, and veracity. Machine learning finds patterns without explicit rules (supervised vs unsupervised); applications include text/sentiment analysis and algorithmic trading. Know the vocabulary at a conceptual level.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Swapping Type I (reject a true H0) and Type II (fail to reject a false one).
  • Reading a high R² as proof of causation — regression shows association, not cause.
  • Saying you "accept H0" — you only ever fail to reject it.

Firms & market structures

A profit-maximizing firm produces the quantity where marginal revenue equals marginal cost. Producing one more unit adds revenue (MR) and cost (MC); while MR > MC keep going, stop when they meet.

profit max:  MR = MC
break-even:  P = ATC   (TR = TC)
short-run shutdown if  P < AVC

The four structures

StructureFirmsPricing powerLong-run profit
Perfect competitionManyNone (price taker, P = MR)Zero economic
Monopolistic competitionMany, differentiatedSomeZero economic
OligopolyFew, interdependentVaries (game theory)Can persist
MonopolyOneHigh (price maker)Can persist (barriers)
Worked example Keep operating or shut down?
  1. A competitive firm faces price P = $8. Its AVC = $6 and ATC = $10.
  2. Short run: P ($8) > AVC ($6), so it covers variable costs plus a bit toward fixed — keep operating.
  3. But P < ATC, so it's losing money; in the long run, if nothing improves, it should exit.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Using ATC for the short-run shutdown test — the short-run test is against AVC.
  • Thinking firms maximize revenue; they maximize profit, where MR = MC.
  • Assuming perfect competitors earn profits long run — entry competes them to zero economic profit.

Business cycles, fiscal & monetary policy

Total output is measured by GDP. The expenditure approach:

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

Economies move through business cycles (expansion → peak → contraction → trough). Unemployment comes in types (frictional, structural, cyclical); inflation is measured by price indexes (CPI) and can be demand-pull or cost-push.

Two levers

Fiscal policy is the government's spending and taxes. Expansionary fiscal policy (more spending, lower taxes) boosts aggregate demand. Monetary policy is the central bank's control of money and interest rates.

simple spending multiplier = 11 − MPC
quantity of money:  M · V = P · Y
nominal rate ≈ real rate + expected inflation
Worked example The spending multiplier
  1. Households spend 80 cents of each extra dollar, so MPC = 0.8.
  2. Multiplier = 1 / (1 − 0.8) = 1 / 0.2.
  3. = 5 — $1 of new government spending can raise output by up to $5 (in the simple model).
Who does what Fiscal policy = legislature/treasury (spending & taxes). Monetary policy = the central bank (rates & money supply). They can reinforce or offset each other.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Mixing up fiscal (government budget) and monetary (central bank) policy.
  • Confusing nominal and real interest rates — strip out expected inflation for the real rate.
3

International trade & geopolitics

Countries gain from trade by specializing according to comparative advantage — producing what they give up the least to make (lowest opportunity cost) — even if another country is better at everything (absolute advantage).

Worked example Comparative advantage
  1. Country A gives up 2 units of cloth to make 1 unit of wine. Country B gives up only 0.5 cloth per wine.
  2. B's opportunity cost of wine (0.5 cloth) is lower than A's (2 cloth).
  3. So B should specialize in wine and A in cloth, then trade — both end up better off.

Trade restrictions — tariffs (a tax on imports), quotas, and subsidies — generally raise the domestic price, protect local producers, and create a deadweight loss for the economy overall. The balance of payments splits into the current account (trade in goods/services) and the capital/financial account. Bodies like the IMF, World Bank, and WTO facilitate trade and stability.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Confusing absolute advantage (being better) with comparative advantage (lower opportunity cost) — trade is driven by the latter.
  • Assuming a tariff lowers prices for consumers; it raises the domestic price.
4

Currency exchange rates

An exchange rate is a price: price currency per unit of base currency (P/B). If USD/EUR = 1.10, the base is the euro and 1 EUR costs 1.10 USD. When that number rises, the base currency (EUR) has appreciated.

% change in base = new rate − old rateold rate
forward premium = forward − spot
covered parity:  FS = 1 + rprice1 + rbase
Worked example Did the euro strengthen?
  1. USD/EUR (price/base) goes from 1.10 to 1.15.
  2. It now takes more dollars to buy one euro.
  3. So the euro (the base) has appreciated by (1.15 − 1.10)/1.10 ≈ 4.5%; the dollar depreciated.
Worked example A cross rate
  1. USD/EUR = 1.10 and USD/GBP = 1.25. Find EUR/GBP.
  2. EUR/GBP = (USD/GBP) ÷ (USD/EUR) = 1.25 / 1.10.
  3. ≈ 1.136 euros per pound.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Mixing up the base and price currency — always identify which is which before computing.
  • Getting the appreciation direction backwards: a rising P/B quote means the base strengthened.
  • Putting the wrong country's rate in the numerator of covered interest rate parity (price currency on top).

The Code of Ethics & the framework

The CFA Institute Code of Ethics is six high-level principles; the Standards of Professional Conduct are seven enforceable rule-sets (I–VII) that put the Code into practice. On the exam, almost every Ethics question is a short vignette where someone acts, and you decide which Standard is at stake and whether conduct was a violation.

The six components of the Code Members and Candidates must: (1) act with integrity, competence, diligence, and respect; (2) place the integrity of the profession and clients' interests above their own; (3) use reasonable care and independent professional judgment; (4) practice and encourage others to practice professionally and ethically; (5) promote the integrity and viability of global capital markets; (6) maintain and improve their professional competence.

The golden rule: follow the stricter standard When the Code/Standards and the applicable law differ, you must comply with the more strict. If local law is silent or less strict, the Code governs. You are never excused because "everyone does it" or because a superior told you to.
Scenario Which rule wins?
  1. An analyst works in a country whose law permits trading on material nonpublic information (MNPI).
  2. The Code/Standards prohibit it (Standard II-A). Law is less strict than the Code.
  3. Apply the stricter rule: do not trade. Local permissiveness is no defense.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Assuming legal = ethical. The Standards can demand more than the law.
  • Thinking intentions matter most — most Standards are about conduct and disclosure, not motive.
  • Forgetting that the Code applies globally, regardless of where you work.
2

Standards I–III

I — Professionalism

I(A) Knowledge of the Law. Understand and comply with all applicable laws/rules/regulations; in a conflict, follow the stricter. Dissociate from any violation you cannot stop. I(B) Independence & Objectivity. Don't let gifts, trips, or pressure compromise judgment; modest token gifts from clients may be acceptable, but lavish ones or gifts from issuers being researched are not. I(C) Misrepresentation. No false statements, no plagiarism, no guaranteeing returns on risky assets. I(D) Misconduct. No dishonesty, fraud, or deceit reflecting on professional reputation (e.g., personal acts like fraud — but not lawful civil disobedience).

II — Integrity of Capital Markets

II(A) Material Nonpublic Information. If information is both material (would affect price / a reasonable investor would want it) and nonpublic, you may not act or cause others to act on it. The mosaic theory is a defense: combining public and non-material nonpublic items into a conclusion is allowed. II(B) Market Manipulation. No transactions or information designed to mislead — including "pump-and-dump," fictitious trades, or spreading false rumors to move prices.

Mosaic theory An analyst may reach a market-moving conclusion by piecing together public data and immaterial non-public scraps — that diligence is rewarded, not punished. Acting on a single material non-public fact is the violation.

III — Duties to Clients

III(A) Loyalty, Prudence & Care. Act for the client's benefit; for whom you owe the duty depends on the relationship (e.g., to plan beneficiaries, not the plan sponsor). "Soft dollars/commissions" must benefit the client. III(B) Fair Dealing. Treat all clients fairly when disseminating recommendations or taking action — don't favor some over others. III(C) Suitability. For advisory relationships, build and update an IPS; ensure recommendations fit the client's objectives and constraints. For an index/mandate, suitability is judged against that mandate. III(D) Performance Presentation. Fair, accurate, complete — no cherry-picking. III(E) Preservation of Confidentiality. Keep client information confidential unless it concerns illegal activity, disclosure is required by law, or the client permits it.

Scenario Fair dealing in practice
  1. A firm upgrades a stock from "hold" to "buy."
  2. Standard III(B) requires reaching all clients for whom it's appropriate at substantially the same time.
  3. Calling the largest accounts first, then emailing the rest hours later, violates fair dealing.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Treating "fair dealing" as "equal dealing" — clients can get different service levels if disclosed; they can't get materially different timing of the same recommendation.
  • Breaking confidentiality for ordinary regulator requests when not legally compelled.
  • Forgetting suitability is anchored to the client's IPS, not the analyst's market view.
3

Standards IV–VII

IV — Duties to Employers

IV(A) Loyalty. Act for your employer's benefit; don't deprive it of your skills, divulge confidential information, or solicit clients before leaving. You may prepare to compete (no firm property/records taken). IV(B) Additional Compensation Arrangements. No gifts/benefits that compete with or conflict with the employer's interest without written consent from all parties. IV(C) Responsibilities of Supervisors. Make reasonable efforts to detect and prevent violations by those you supervise; an adequate compliance system must exist and be used.

V — Investment Analysis, Recommendations & Actions

V(A) Diligence & Reasonable Basis. Have adequate research support; when using others' (or third-party) research, verify it's sound. V(B) Communication with Clients. Distinguish fact from opinion, disclose the basic process and its limits/risks, and report material changes. V(C) Record Retention. Keep records supporting analysis (CFA Institute recommends a minimum of seven years absent other regulation); records are the firm's property.

VI — Conflicts of Interest

VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts. Prominently disclose anything that could impair objectivity — ownership in a recommended security, broker/banking relationships, etc. VI(B) Priority of Transactions. Client transactions come before the firm's, which come before an employee's (clients > employer > self). VI(C) Referral Fees. Disclose to clients/employer any compensation for referrals so they can judge the cost and any partiality.

VII — Responsibilities as a Member/Candidate

VII(A) Conduct in the CFA Program. Don't cheat, reveal exam content, or violate testing rules. (You may express opinions about CFA Institute or the program.) VII(B) Reference to CFA Institute, the Designation & the Program. No exaggeration; the charter is not a guarantee of performance. State it correctly ("I am a CFA charterholder"); "CFA" is an adjective, not a noun, and there is no partial designation for passing a level.

Scenario Stating the designation
  1. A candidate who passed Level II writes "CFA Level II" after her name on a business card.
  2. Standard VII(B): there is no designation for a partial pass; "CFA" cannot follow a name until the charter is awarded.
  3. She may state she "passed Levels I and II" — but the card as written violates VII(B).
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Soliciting an employer's clients before resigning (IV-A) — even "warm" relationships are off-limits while employed.
  • Reversing priority of transactions — clients always come first, then employer, then self.
  • Writing "CFA" as a noun or implying it predicts returns (VII-B).
4

GIPS — Global Investment Performance Standards

GIPS are voluntary, ethical standards for calculating and presenting investment performance so that results are fair, comparable, and complete across firms and across borders. They exist to end misleading practices like cherry-picking winning accounts or "survivorship" reporting.

Who can claim compliance Only an investment-management firm — defined as a distinct business entity — can claim GIPS compliance, and it must apply the standards firm-wide. You cannot claim compliance for a single composite, product, or account. Compliance is all-or-nothing; partial compliance cannot be claimed.

Key building blocks

  • Composites. All actual, fee-paying, discretionary portfolios with a similar strategy must be grouped into a composite — this prevents showing only the winners.
  • Fundamentals. Input data integrity, time-weighted returns, total return (including realized and unrealized gains plus income), and a minimum required history (build toward 10 years).
  • Verification. A separate, third-party check of firm-wide processes; it is recommended but not required, and it applies to the whole firm, not a single composite.
Nine sections of GIPS Fundamentals of compliance; input data; calculation methodology; composite construction; disclosure; presentation & reporting; real estate; private equity; wrap-fee/SMA portfolios. You need the concepts and the "who/why," not line-item recall, at Level I.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Saying a single product "is GIPS compliant" — only the firm can be, firm-wide.
  • Treating verification as mandatory — it's recommended, and it covers the firm, not one composite.
  • Excluding fee-paying discretionary accounts from a composite — that's exactly what GIPS forbids.

The statements & the analysis framework

Four statements describe a company. The balance sheet is a snapshot at a point in time; the income statement, cash flow statement, and statement of changes in equity cover a period. The notes and MD&A explain the choices behind the numbers, and the auditor's opinion tells you how much to trust them.

The accounting equation
Assets = Liabilities + Owners' equity
Equity = contributed capital + retained earnings − treasury stock + OCI

How the statements link (the articulation)

Net income flows from the income statement into retained earnings on the balance sheet (less dividends) and is the starting line of the indirect cash flow statement. Ending cash on the cash flow statement equals the cash line on the balance sheet. Master this chain — most FSA questions test a link in it.

Accrual accounting Revenue is recognized when earned and expenses when incurred (matched to revenue), not when cash moves. That's why a profitable company can run out of cash, and why the cash flow statement matters as much as earnings.

IFRS vs US GAAP — the framing

Both aim for relevant, faithful representation. Differences appear throughout (inventory LIFO, development cost capitalization, revaluation of assets, interest/dividend classification in cash flows). At Level I, know the direction of the major differences rather than every detail.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Confusing the snapshot (balance sheet) with the flow statements (everything else).
  • Equating net income with cash — accruals break that link.
  • Ignoring the notes, where the accounting policies that drive the numbers actually live.

Income statement & EPS

The income statement runs from revenue down to net income, peeling off costs along the way. Know the margin ladder and where each line sits.

Gross profit = Revenue − COGS
Operating income (EBIT) = Gross profit − operating expenses
Net income = EBIT − interest − taxes (± non-operating items)

Revenue recognition (5-step model)

Under the converged standard: (1) identify the contract, (2) identify the performance obligations, (3) determine the transaction price, (4) allocate price to obligations, (5) recognize revenue as each obligation is satisfied. The point: revenue is tied to delivering the promise, not to billing or collecting.

Earnings per share

Basic EPS
Basic EPS = Net income − preferred dividendsweighted-avg common shares
Diluted EPS
Diluted = NI − pref. div + after-tax conv. interest + conv. pref. divweighted shares + all dilutive potential shares
Worked example Basic EPS with a mid-year issue
  1. Net income $2,000,000; preferred dividends $200,000.
  2. 1,000,000 shares outstanding all year; 200,000 more issued July 1 → weighted = 1,000,000 + 200,000 × (6/12) = 1,100,000.
  3. Basic EPS = (2,000,000 − 200,000) / 1,100,000 = $1.64.
Antidilutive securities are excluded Only convertible securities and options that reduce EPS are included in diluted EPS. Anything that would raise EPS is ignored (you never report a diluted figure above basic). Options enter via the treasury-stock method.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting to subtract preferred dividends in basic EPS.
  • Using year-end shares instead of the weighted-average.
  • Including antidilutive instruments in diluted EPS.

Balance sheet & cash flow

The balance sheet classifies assets and liabilities as current (within one year/operating cycle) or non-current. Measurement mixes historical cost, amortized cost, and fair value. Marketable securities are split into categories whose gains hit either net income or OCI.

Direct vs indirect CFO Both give the same CFO total. The direct method lists actual cash receipts/payments; the indirect method starts at net income and adds back non-cash items (depreciation), then adjusts for working-capital changes. The indirect method is far more common and heavily tested.

FCFF = CFO + Int(1 − tax) − FCInv

  2. Start at NI, add back non-cash depreciation: 500 + 80 = 580.
  3. A/R up $40 is a use of cash (−40); A/P up $25 is a source (+25). CFO = 580 − 40 + 25 = $565.

  • Sign errors on working capital: an increase in a current asset uses cash; an increase in a current liability provides cash.
  • Putting interest paid in the wrong section — under US GAAP it's in CFO; IFRS allows CFO or CFF.
  • Confusing FCFF (to all capital) with FCFE (to equity only).

Inventories, long-lived assets & income taxes

Inventory cost flow

The same physical goods produce different earnings depending on the cost-flow assumption. In rising prices: FIFO leaves newer, higher costs on the balance sheet (higher inventory) and older, lower costs in COGS (higher income); LIFO does the reverse (lower income, lower taxes, but understated inventory). Weighted average sits between.

Long-lived assets

Capitalize vs expense: capitalizing a cost puts it on the balance sheet and spreads it through depreciation — boosting current income and assets, smoothing later years. Expensing front-loads the hit. Depreciation methods: straight-line is even; accelerated (e.g., double-declining) front-loads expense, lowering early income. Impairment writes an asset down when its carrying value exceeds recoverable amount (reversible under IFRS, generally not under US GAAP).

Income taxes (deferred)

Accounting income and taxable income differ because of temporary differences (e.g., depreciation method). A deferred tax liability arises when book income > taxable income now (you'll pay later); a deferred tax asset when you've paid tax on income not yet booked. Permanent differences (e.g., tax-exempt interest) never reverse and instead change the effective tax rate.

Financial reporting quality High quality = decision-useful and sustainable earnings. Watch for aggressive revenue timing, capitalizing what should be expensed, and "one-time" charges that recur. Quality of earnings > quantity of earnings.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting that in rising prices FIFO gives higher income but LIFO gives lower taxes.
  • Treating an impairment reversal as allowed under US GAAP for held assets — it generally is not.
  • Confusing temporary differences (create deferred taxes) with permanent ones (change the effective rate).

Corporate structures & governance

A corporation separates ownership (shareholders) from control (managers). That separation creates the principal–agent problem: managers may pursue their own interests (empire-building, perks) over shareholders'. Governance is the system of controls that aligns them.

Stakeholders Beyond shareholders: debtholders, managers, employees, customers, suppliers, governments. Each has different claims and time horizons — e.g., debtholders want safety and oppose risky bets that equity holders might favor. Governance balances these.

ESG

Environmental, social, and governance factors are increasingly built into analysis: environmental (emissions, resource use), social (labor, safety, data privacy), governance (board independence, shareholder rights). ESG can be a source of risk (litigation, stranded assets) and is integrated through screening, tilts, or active ownership.

Business models A business model describes how a firm creates and captures value — who the customer is, the channel, pricing (subscription, freemium, razor-and-blades), and the value proposition. It frames everything else in the analysis.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Assuming shareholder and debtholder interests align — they often conflict over risk.
  • Treating ESG as only ethics rather than a lens on long-run risk and return.
2

Capital investments (NPV & IRR)

Capital budgeting decides which long-term projects to fund. Use incremental, after-tax cash flows; ignore sunk costs; include opportunity costs and externalities.

Net present value
NPV = −CF0 + Σ CFt(1 + r)t
Accept if NPV > 0 — it adds value at the required return r
Internal rate of return
0 = −CF0 + Σ CFt(1 + IRR)t
Accept if IRR > required return; the rate that sets NPV = 0
Worked example A two-year project
  1. Invest $1,000 now; receive $600 in year 1 and $700 in year 2; required return 10%.
  2. NPV = −1000 + 600/1.10 + 700/1.21 = −1000 + 545.45 + 578.51.
  3. = +$123.96 → accept. (NPV > 0 means IRR > 10%.)
NPV vs IRR conflicts For independent projects with normal cash flows they agree. For mutually exclusive projects or unconventional cash flows they can disagree (IRR can be multiple or misleading). NPV is the superior rule because it measures value added in currency and uses the right reinvestment assumption.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Including sunk costs or financing costs in project cash flows (financing is in the discount rate).
  • Choosing the higher-IRR project when NPV ranks them differently — follow NPV.
  • Forgetting opportunity costs and cannibalization (negative externalities).

Cost of capital & leverage

The weighted-average cost of capital (WACC) is the blended required return on a firm's financing — the hurdle rate for average-risk projects. Use market-value weights and the after-tax cost of debt (interest is tax-deductible).

WACC
WACC = wdrd(1 − t) + wprp + were

Estimating the cost of equity

CAPM
re = Rf + β(Rm − Rf)

Worked example Computing WACC
  1. 40% debt, 60% equity. Pre-tax cost of debt 6%, tax rate 25%, cost of equity 12%.
  2. After-tax debt = 6% × (1 − 0.25) = 4.5%.
  3. WACC = 0.40 × 4.5% + 0.60 × 12% = 1.8% + 7.2% = 9.0%.

Leverage

Operating leverage (fixed operating costs) and financial leverage (fixed financing costs) both magnify how earnings respond to a change in sales.

DOL = % change in operating income% change in units sold   DFL = % change in net income% change in operating income
DTL = DOL × DFL   breakeven Q = fixed costsprice − variable cost
Modigliani–Miller (intuition) Without taxes or distress costs, capital structure doesn't change firm value — value comes from assets, not financing. With tax-deductible interest, debt adds a tax shield that raises value, but rising bankruptcy and agency costs eventually offset it, implying an optimal mix.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Using the pre-tax cost of debt in WACC — it must be after-tax.
  • Using book-value weights instead of market-value weights.
  • Confusing operating leverage (cost structure) with financial leverage (debt).
4

Working capital & liquidity

Working capital management keeps the firm solvent in the short run — funding receivables and inventory while paying suppliers. The goal is to shorten the time cash is tied up without starving operations.

Cash conversion cycle
CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO
days receivables + days inventory − days payables

A shorter cash conversion cycle frees cash. Sources of short-term funding include lines of credit, commercial paper (for strong issuers), and trade credit. Liquidity ratios (current, quick) and the firm's drag (uncollected receivables) and pull (paying too early) on liquidity matter.

Worked example Cash conversion cycle
  1. DSO = 40 days, DIO = 60 days, DPO = 30 days.
  2. CCC = 40 + 60 − 30.
  3. = 70 days that cash is tied up before it returns. Cutting DSO or DIO releases cash.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Adding DPO instead of subtracting it — paying suppliers later shortens the cycle.
  • Chasing the shortest cycle at the cost of stockouts or lost sales.

Equity Investments

How share markets are organized, what it means for prices to be "efficient," the kinds of equity you can own, and the three families of valuation — discounted dividends, multiples, and asset-based — that turn a business into a price.

1

Market organization, indexes & efficiency

Financial markets let savers and borrowers meet, set prices, and provide liquidity. Securities trade in primary markets (issuance: IPOs, seasoned offerings) and secondary markets (existing shares). Positions can be long or short; margin magnifies gains and losses, with a margin call when equity falls below the maintenance level.

Index construction & weighting

WeightingHowWatch-out
Price-weightedSum of prices ÷ divisorHigh-priced stocks dominate; splits distort
Market-cap (value)By float-adjusted market valueOverweights large & possibly overvalued names
Equal-weightedSame weight eachNeeds frequent rebalancing; small-cap tilt

Market efficiency (EMH)

An efficient market reflects information in prices quickly, so consistently beating it (after costs) is hard. The three forms: weak (prices reflect past prices → technical analysis can't add value), semi-strong (reflect all public information → fundamental analysis on public data can't reliably add value), strong (reflect even private information → not supported empirically). Anomalies and behavioral biases push back at the edges.

Why efficiency matters If markets are semi-strong efficient, the average active manager can't beat the index after fees — the case for indexing. Pockets of inefficiency are where active management hopes to earn its keep.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Thinking a price-weighted index measures "the market" — it's skewed by share price, not size.
  • Believing semi-strong efficiency rules out all profit — it rules out reliably profiting from public info.
2

Equity securities & company analysis

Common shares carry residual ownership and usually votes; preferred shares pay a set dividend and rank ahead of common but typically lack votes (can be cumulative, participating, convertible, callable). Depositary receipts (ADRs/GDRs) give exposure to foreign shares.

Cost of equity ≈ investors' required return
accounting ROE = net incomeavg book equity   book value of equity = assets − liabilities

Industry & company analysis

Frame the industry (life-cycle stage, concentration) then the company. Porter's five forces — rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, supplier power, buyer power — gauge how much profit an industry can sustain. A firm competes on cost leadership or differentiation; a durable edge ("moat") is what protects returns above the cost of capital.

Sustainable growth rate Growth a firm can fund from retained earnings without new equity: g = retention ratio × ROE = (1 − payout) × ROE. It links profitability and dividend policy to how fast the business — and its dividends — can grow.
g = b × ROE = (1 − payout ratio) × ROE
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Treating preferred stock as debt — it's equity, though it behaves bond-like.
  • Forgetting g depends on retained earnings; a 100% payout means g = 0 from internal funds.
3

Valuation: dividend discount models

An asset is worth the present value of its future cash flows. For equity, that's dividends (DDM) or free cash flow to equity (FCFE), discounted at the required return on equity.

Gordon (constant-growth) model
V0 = D1r − g = D0(1 + g)r − g
requires r > g and a stable, perpetual growth rate
Worked example Gordon growth value
  1. Next year's dividend D1 = $2.00; required return 10%; growth 4%.
  2. V0 = 2.00 / (0.10 − 0.04) = 2.00 / 0.06.
  3. = $33.33. Note how sensitive this is to (r − g): a 1% change in g moves value sharply.

Multistage & FCFE

When growth isn't constant, value the high-growth dividends explicitly, then apply Gordon to a terminal value at the point growth stabilizes, and discount everything back. The H-model approximates a linearly declining growth rate. FCFE models suit firms that pay no/erratic dividends — value the cash available to shareholders.

Terminal value (at year n)
Vn = Dn+1r − gstable
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Using D0 instead of D1 in the numerator — the model discounts next year's dividend.
  • Setting g ≥ r, which makes the formula meaningless (implies infinite value).
  • Applying constant-growth DDM to a firm whose growth clearly isn't stable.
4

Valuation: price multiples

Multiples value a company relative to peers: cheap or dear versus comparable businesses. They're fast and market-based but only as good as the comparables.

Common multiples
P/E = priceEPS   P/B = pricebook value/share   P/S = pricesales/share
Justified leading P/E (from Gordon)
P0E1 = payout ratior − g

Leading (forward) multiples use expected next-year earnings; trailing use the last 12 months. Enterprise-value multiples (EV/EBITDA) are useful across different capital structures because EV includes debt and EBITDA precedes interest.

Worked example Justified P/E
  1. Payout ratio 40%, required return 9%, growth 4%.
  2. Justified leading P/E = 0.40 / (0.09 − 0.04) = 0.40 / 0.05.
  3. = 8.0×. If the stock trades at 12× on these fundamentals, it looks rich.
Earnings quality and multiples A low P/E can be a value bargain or a warning that earnings are about to fall ("value trap"). Always pair a multiple with the growth, risk, and accounting quality behind it.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Comparing P/E across firms with very different growth, risk, or leverage.
  • Using EBITDA multiples while ignoring real capital-spending needs.
  • Trusting a multiple built on low-quality or one-off earnings.

Fixed Income

The largest asset class in the world. How a bond is built, how its price comes straight from discounting promised cash flows, the yield measures that summarize it, and how duration and convexity tell you what a rate move will do to the price.

1 · Features & structure 2 · Pricing & yields 3 · Duration & convexity 4 · Spreads, securitization & credit
1

Bond features & structure

A bond is a contractual loan. Its indenture specifies the par (face) value, coupon (rate and frequency), maturity, seniority, and any embedded options. Coupons may be fixed, floating (reference rate + spread), or zero.

Price vs parWhenName
Price < parcoupon < market yieldDiscount bond
Price = parcoupon = market yieldPar bond
Price > parcoupon > market yieldPremium bond
Embedded options change who benefits A callable bond lets the issuer redeem early (good for the issuer when rates fall) → investor demands a higher yield. A putable bond lets the investor sell back early (good for the investor) → lower yield. A convertible can become equity.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Thinking a premium bond is "better" — its price simply pulls to par as maturity approaches.
  • Forgetting a call option raises the yield investors require.
2

Pricing & yield measures

A bond's price is just the present value of its coupons plus par, discounted at the market yield. There is an inverse, convex relationship between price and yield: yields up, price down.

Price (single discount rate)
P = Σ coupon(1 + y)t + par(1 + y)N
Yield shortcuts
current yield = annual couponprice
YTM = the single rate that makes PV of cash flows = price (assumes reinvestment at YTM and holding to maturity)

Spot rates discount a single future cash flow; a bond's "true" value uses the spot curve. The YTM is a complex average of spot rates. For callable bonds, compute yield-to-worst (the lowest of yield-to-call dates and YTM).

Worked example Price a 2-year annual bond
  1. Par $100, 5% annual coupon, market yield 6%.
  2. P = 5/1.06 + 105/1.06² = 4.717 + 93.466.
  3. = $98.18 — a discount, because coupon (5%) < yield (6%).
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Confusing current yield (coupon/price) with YTM (the full internal rate).
  • Assuming YTM is realized regardless of reinvestment — it assumes coupons reinvest at YTM.
  • Forgetting price and yield move in opposite directions.
3

Duration & convexity

Duration measures interest-rate risk: roughly the % price change for a 1% (100 bp) change in yield. Macaulay duration is the weighted-average time to receive cash flows; modified duration scales it to a price sensitivity; effective duration is used when cash flows can change (bonds with options).

Modified duration & price impact
ModDur = MacDur1 + y/k
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × Δy
Adding convexity (the curvature term)
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × Δy + ½ × convexity × (Δy)2
Worked example Price change from a rate move
  1. Modified duration 7.0; convexity 60; yields rise 50 bp (Δy = +0.005).
  2. Duration term = −7.0 × 0.005 = −3.50%.
  3. Convexity term = ½ × 60 × (0.005)² = +0.075%. Total ≈ −3.43% — convexity softens the drop.
Convexity is the investor's friend Because the price–yield curve bows, duration overstates the price fall when rates rise and understates the rise when rates fall. The convexity term corrects both, always in the investor's favor for an option-free bond. Longer maturity, lower coupon, and lower yield all raise duration.
Money duration & PVBP
money duration = ModDur × price   PVBP = money duration × 0.0001
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Dropping the minus sign — higher yields lower price.
  • Using modified duration on a callable/putable bond — use effective duration.
  • Ignoring convexity for large yield moves, where it matters most.
4

Spreads, securitization & credit

A bond's yield = a benchmark (government/swap) rate + a spread that compensates for credit risk, liquidity, and options. Wider spreads mean more perceived risk.

yield = benchmark + spread
G-spread (vs govt), I-spread (vs swap), Z-spread (constant over spot curve), OAS (Z-spread minus option cost)

Credit risk

Credit risk is the chance of default and the loss if it happens. Expected loss = probability of default × loss given default, where loss given default = 1 − recovery rate. Ratings (investment grade vs high yield) summarize an issuer's credit quality. The "4 Cs": capacity, collateral, covenants, character.

Securitization

Pools of loans (mortgages, auto loans, receivables) are sold to a special purpose entity that issues asset-backed securities in tranches with different priority. This redistributes cash flows and risk; mortgage-backed securities carry prepayment risk (borrowers refinance when rates fall, returning principal at the worst time for investors).

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Treating the spread as pure credit risk — it also pays for liquidity and embedded options.
  • Forgetting prepayment risk shortens MBS just when reinvestment rates are low.
  • Ignoring that OAS strips out the option cost so option-bearing bonds can be compared fairly.

Derivatives

Contracts whose value is derived from something else. The four building blocks — forwards, futures, swaps, options — and the one big idea that prices them all: arbitrage. If two portfolios pay the same in every state, they must cost the same today.

1 · Instruments & markets 2 · Forward/futures pricing 3 · Options & put–call parity
1

Derivative instruments & markets

A derivative's payoff depends on an underlying (a stock, index, rate, currency, or commodity). They're used to hedge risk, gain cheap exposure, or arbitrage mispricing.

TypeObligation?Traded
ForwardBoth sides obligated; customOTC (counterparty risk)
FutureBoth obligated; standardizedExchange (cleared, margined, marked-to-market daily)
SwapSeries of exchanges (≈ forwards)OTC
OptionBuyer has the right, not obligation; pays a premiumExchange or OTC
Forwards vs futures Economically similar, but futures are exchange-traded, standardized, and marked to market daily through a clearinghouse — which largely removes counterparty risk and creates daily cash flows. Forwards are customizable OTC contracts that settle once, at expiry.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Saying an option buyer is "obligated" — only the writer (seller) is obligated.
  • Ignoring counterparty/credit risk in forwards and swaps (mitigated for futures by the clearinghouse).
2

Forward & futures pricing

The forward price is set so that no arbitrage is possible: buying the asset and carrying it must cost the same as agreeing to buy it forward. This is the cost-of-carry model.

Forward price (no income)
F0 = S0(1 + r)T
With carry benefits (income, γ) and costs (θ)
F0 = (S0 − PV(income) + PV(cost))(1 + r)T
benefits (dividends, convenience yield) lower F; costs (storage) raise F
Worked example A 1-year forward on a non-dividend stock
  1. Spot S0 = $100; risk-free rate 5%; T = 1 year; no dividends.
  2. F0 = 100 × (1.05)1.
  3. = $105. If the forward traded at $108, you'd short it, buy the stock, and lock a riskless $3.
Value vs price of a forward At initiation the contract's value is zero (the price is set fairly). As the spot moves, the long's value = the gain relative to the locked price, discounted. A swap is just a bundle of forwards priced so the initial value is zero.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Adding income to the forward price — asset income reduces the forward price.
  • Confusing the forward price (set at the start) with the forward value (changes over time).
3

Options & put–call parity

A call gives the right to buy at the strike X; a put gives the right to sell at X. Buyers pay a premium and risk only that premium; writers receive it and bear the open-ended risk.

Payoffs at expiration
call = max(0, ST − X)   put = max(0, X − ST)

Option value = intrinsic value (in-the-money amount, never below 0) + time value (which decays to zero at expiry). Call value rises with the underlying price, volatility, time, and the risk-free rate; it falls with a higher strike. Higher volatility raises both call and put values.

Put–call parity
S0 + p = c + X(1 + r)T
a protective put = a fiduciary call; if they cost differently, arbitrage exists
Worked example Solve for a put with parity
  1. S0 = $50, call c = $4, strike X = $50, PV(X) = $48.
  2. p = c + PV(X) − S0 = 4 + 48 − 50.
  3. = $2. Parity lets you price one option from the other.
One-period binomial Build a riskless hedge of stock and option; its return must equal the risk-free rate. The option value uses risk-neutral probabilities π = [(1 + r) − d] / (u − d), discounting the expected payoff at r. The real-world probability of an up move never enters.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Thinking higher volatility helps only calls — it raises put values too.
  • Misremembering parity — protective put (S + p) equals fiduciary call (c + PV(X)).
  • Using real-world rather than risk-neutral probabilities in the binomial model.

Alternative Investments

Everything outside traditional stocks, bonds, and cash: private equity, real estate, hedge funds, commodities, and infrastructure. The appeal is diversification and return potential; the cost is illiquidity, complex fees, and valuation that isn't a daily market price.

1 · Categories & features 2 · Structures, fees & returns 3 · Valuation & commodities
1

Categories & features

Alternatives share a profile: lower liquidity, longer horizons, less regulation and transparency, higher fees, and returns that depend heavily on the manager. They're held for diversification (low correlation with stocks/bonds) and return potential.

CategoryWhat it is
Private equityBuyouts (LBOs) and venture capital — owning private companies
Real estateDirect property, REITs, mortgages — income + appreciation
Hedge fundsFlexible strategies (long/short, macro, event-driven, relative value)
CommoditiesPhysical goods, usually via futures — inflation hedge
InfrastructureLong-lived public assets (roads, utilities) — stable cash flows
Why correlations matter (and a caution) Alternatives are prized for low correlation with public markets — but reported correlations can be understated because valuations are appraisal-based and infrequent ("smoothed"), which also understates true volatility. Real diversification may be smaller than the statistics suggest.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Taking reported volatility/correlation at face value — stale, appraised prices smooth them.
  • Assuming liquidity — most alternatives lock capital up for years.
2

Structures, fees & returns

Most alternatives use a partnership: a general partner (GP) runs it; limited partners (LPs) provide capital. Compensation is the classic "2 and 20" — a management fee on assets plus an incentive fee on profits.

Hedge-fund fee (per period)
fee = (mgmt% × assets) + (incentive% × profit above hurdle / high-water mark)
Hurdle rate & high-water mark A hurdle means incentive fees apply only to returns above a threshold. A high-water mark means the manager earns incentive fees only on new profits above the prior peak — so investors don't pay twice to recover the same losses.
Worked example "2 and 20" with a high-water mark
  1. Start $100M; 2% management fee (on year-end value); 20% incentive on gains above the high-water mark; gross return +20% → $120M.
  2. Management fee = 2% × 120 = $2.4M. Incentive = 20% × (120 − 100) = $4.0M.
  3. Total fees ≈ $6.4M; net to investor ≈ $113.6M. Next year, fees accrue only above $120M.

Private equity J-curve: early years show negative returns (fees + write-downs) before exits drive gains, so the cumulative return curve dips then rises. PE value is created via operational improvement, leverage, and multiple expansion at exit.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the high-water mark — incentive fees don't restart until prior losses are recovered.
  • Computing the incentive fee on total assets rather than on profit above the hurdle/mark.
  • Judging a young PE fund by its early (J-curve) returns.
3

Valuation & commodities

Without daily market prices, alternatives are valued by models. Real estate uses the income (cap-rate), cost, and comparable-sales approaches. PE uses comparable multiples or discounted cash flow at entry/exit.

Real-estate capitalization
value = net operating income (NOI)cap rate   cap rate = NOIproperty value

Commodities & the futures return

Most commodity exposure is via futures, so the total return has three parts: the spot price change, the roll yield from rolling expiring contracts, and the collateral yield on the cash backing the position.

futures return ≈ spot return + roll yield + collateral yield
Contango vs backwardation Contango: futures price > spot → rolling forward sells low-priced expiring contracts and buys higher-priced ones → negative roll yield. Backwardation: futures < spot → positive roll yield. This roll effect can dominate long-run commodity returns.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Thinking commodity returns are just spot price moves — roll and collateral yields matter, often more.
  • Reversing contango/backwardation — contango (upward curve) tends to drag returns via negative roll.
  • Dividing NOI by the wrong rate — a lower cap rate implies a higher value.

Portfolio Management

Where every other topic comes together. Risk is managed at the portfolio level, not security by security: how combining assets reduces risk, where the best risk–return trade-offs live, how CAPM prices systematic risk, and how to plan and measure a portfolio.

1

The portfolio process & types of investors

Portfolio management runs in three stages: planning (understand the investor, write the IPS), execution (asset allocation, security selection), and feedback (monitor, rebalance, evaluate). The portfolio perspective is the core insight — judge an asset by its contribution to portfolio risk, not in isolation.

InvestorRisk toleranceHorizon / liquidity
IndividualVariesVaries; life-cycle driven
Pension (DB)HigherLong; liquidity from contributions
BankLowerShort; high liquidity needs
Endowment/foundationHigherVery long; spending-rate driven
InsuranceLow (life) / variesTied to liabilities
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Evaluating a security's risk on its own instead of its marginal effect on the portfolio.
  • Assuming all institutions are alike — banks need liquidity; endowments tolerate illiquidity.
2

Risk, return & the efficient frontier

Combining assets that aren't perfectly correlated lowers risk without proportionally lowering return — diversification. The covariance term is what does the work.

Two-asset portfolio
Rp = w1R1 + w2R2
σp2 = w12σ12 + w22σ22 + 2w1w2ρ12σ1σ2
Worked example Diversification benefit
  1. Two assets, σ = 20% each, equal weights, correlation ρ = 0.
  2. σp² = 0.5²(0.2²) + 0.5²(0.2²) + 0 = 0.02 → σp = √0.02.
  3. = 14.1%, well below the 20% average — that gap is diversification.
Efficient frontier & the CAL The efficient frontier holds portfolios with the highest return for each level of risk. Add a risk-free asset and the best mix is where a line from Rf is tangent to the frontier — the capital allocation line. The tangency portfolio (everyone's optimal risky mix) is the market portfolio, giving the capital market line.
Capital market line
Rp = Rf + Rm − Rfσm · σp
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Forgetting portfolio risk is less than the weighted-average SD whenever ρ < 1.
  • Adding standard deviations — you must use the variance formula with covariance.
3

CAPM, beta & performance measures

Diversification removes unsystematic (firm-specific) risk; what remains is systematic (market) risk, measured by beta. CAPM says only systematic risk is rewarded.

Beta & CAPM (the security market line)
βi = cov(i,m)σm2 = ρim σiσm
E(Ri) = Rf + βi(Rm − Rf)
Worked example Required return via CAPM
  1. Risk-free 3%, market return 9%, beta 1.3.
  2. E(R) = 3% + 1.3 × (9% − 3%) = 3% + 1.3 × 6%.
  3. = 10.8%. A stock expected to return more than this plots above the SML — undervalued.

Performance measures

Sharpe = Rp − Rfσp   Treynor = Rp − Rfβp
Jensen's α = Rp − [Rf + βp(Rm − Rf)]
Which ratio when? Sharpe uses total risk (σ) — judge a stand-alone portfolio. Treynor and Jensen's alpha use beta (systematic risk) — judge a sub-portfolio that's part of a larger diversified whole. Positive alpha = beating the return CAPM required for that beta.
⚠ Common mistakes
  • Using Sharpe when only systematic risk is relevant (use Treynor/alpha) and vice versa.
  • Calling a high return "good" without adjusting for the risk taken to earn it.
  • Forgetting beta of the market is 1.0 and the risk-free asset is 0.
4

Planning, the IPS & behavioral finance

The investment policy statement (IPS) is the portfolio's governing document. It captures objectives (return and risk tolerance) and constraints, summarized as R-R-T-T-L-L-U: Return, Risk, then Time horizon, Taxes, Liquidity, Legal/regulatory, and Unique circumstances.

Risk: ability vs willingness Risk tolerance blends the ability to take risk (horizon, wealth, liquidity needs) with the willingness (psychological comfort). When they conflict, the prudent practice is to take the lower of the two and educate the client.

Behavioral finance

Real investors deviate from rationality. Cognitive errors (faulty reasoning — anchoring, confirmation, availability) can be reduced with information and discipline. Emotional biases (loss aversion, overconfidence, regret, herding) are harder to correct and are often accommodated. These biases help explain market anomalies and bubbles.

⚠ Common mistakes
  • Setting risk tolerance from willingness alone, ignoring the client's actual ability (or vice versa).
  • Treating the IPS as paperwork rather than the binding plan that drives allocation.
  • Confusing cognitive errors (correctable) with emotional biases (usually accommodated).
